01

History

Security Shutter becomes Security Molding.

Before it was a plastics company, Security Molding was a shutter company. Lynn Ricky — Julian's great-grandfather — and his father ran Security Shutter out of a small shop on Interstate, a couple blocks from where Jeannie works today. The product: aluminum roll-up shutters that stored above the window and rolled down at night for security.

The shutters rolled on plastic gears. To make those gears, the shop bought plastic molding machines. Those machines are the seed that turned a shutter business into a plastics business. Eventually, the shutters went away. The machines stayed.

Somewhere in the late 70s, Gary — your dad — started coming in on Saturdays. Around 9 years old. Cleaning bathrooms. Separating parts off of runners all day. That's the job that teaches you the most.

A 1970s home with roll-down Security Shutters. The plastic gears that rolled these up and down are the reason Security Molding exists.

"They made the shutters, but they also bought these plastics machines to make the plastic gears that were used to roll the shutters up and down. That's the kind of the beginning of security molding." — Gary, recounting the origin story

02

Machines

Small, vertical, Arburg.

Most plastic shops you'll walk into are full of horizontal machines — huge things where a forklift drops the mold in from the side. Security Molding is different. We run small, vertical, German-built Arburg machines. Vertical means the barrel points straight down. The mold closes sideways. We buy them horizontal and convert them. That's our whole niche.

The fleet

28-ton & 40-ton

Tonnage is how hard the machine can hold the mold shut when plastic is blasting in. We play in the small-tonnage league. Big shops run 500-ton monsters for bumpers and buckets. We run small machines, small molds, small parts, tight cycle times.

03

The Mold

A-side. B-side. Water. Pins.

The mold is the shape-maker. It's two hunks of steel that come together, plastic gets shot in, plastic cools, and two halves open to drop the parts. But inside those two hunks is where all the craft lives — the cavities, the pins, the water lines, the gates. This is where a good mold maker earns their money.

The A-side (stationary, left) is the mirror-polished face with the sprue. The B-side (moving, right) has ejector pins and water-cooling channels snaking through the steel. In between: the sprue, runner, and parts.

Click a side to learn more

The A-side — stationary half

This side doesn't move. It has the sprue — the hole where the nozzle meets the mold. Plastic enters here and flows into the runner. The cavity face on this side is usually polished mirror-smooth so the part releases cleanly and stays on the B-side when the mold opens.

Good mold makers grind a tiny angle on the A-side walls — a slight taper — so the part naturally slides off as the mold opens. You can't see the angle. It's there.

The B-side — moving half

This side opens and closes with the machine. The B-side has the ejector pins — thin steel cylinders flush with the cavity wall during the shot. When the mold opens, something (a pin in the back, or a hydraulic cylinder on newer machines) slams the pin plate forward and the pins push the parts out.

The B-side is harder to build. The water channels have to weave around the pins. Pin hits water = disaster. That's the mold maker's puzzle.

Cooling water — both sides

Drilled channels run through both halves of the mold. Water flows continuously — not draining, circulating. By the time it loops back to the mold, it's cooled down enough to keep pulling heat out of the steel.

Quick-connect fittings on the side of the mold plug into the machine's water supply. Forget to turn on the water? Parts won't cool. Parts won't cool? Ejector pins push straight through the part and you have a very stuck mold.

Term · tap Sprue Where plastic enters
Definition

The hole on the A-side where the machine nozzle meets the mold. First place the plastic hits.

Term · tap Runner Highway for plastic
Definition

The channels that carry molten plastic from the sprue to each cavity. Leftover plastic in the runners gets broken off or cut — then ground up and reused.

Term · tap Tunnel Gate The magic cut
Definition

An angled knife-edge between runner and cavity. As the part ejects, the angle automatically shears the runner off. Saves labor. Clean break. Beautiful design when it works.

Term · tap Ejector Pin Pushes parts out
Definition

Thin steel rod on the B-side, flush during the shot. When the mold opens, the pin plate slams forward and pins push the cooled part out of the cavity.

Term · tap Cavity Part-shaped hole
Definition

The hollow space inside the mold that defines the part's shape. One mold can have 1, 4, 8, 15+ cavities. More cavities = more parts per shot.

Term · tap EDM Burning steel with sparks
Definition

Electrical Discharge Machining. A shaped carbon electrode sits in oil above the steel. Electric sparks burn the shape into the steel. Magic for complex cavities.

Sidebar · EDM

How mold makers carve the impossible

Some cavity shapes can't be drilled. They're too angular, too deep, too weird. Enter EDM — Electrical Discharge Machining.

A mold maker shapes the inverse of the cavity in a block of carbon. They suspend that electrode over the steel in a tank of oil. Then they fire thousands of tiny electric sparks between the carbon and the steel. Each spark burns off a microscopic bit of metal. Bzzzt. Bzzzt. Hours or days later, the cavity is there.

You can design shapes with EDM that are physically impossible to machine with a drill bit. It's the move when your mold needs something clever.

04

The Cycle

Fifteen seconds. A shot. A part.

Every 15 to 30 seconds, the whole cycle happens. The mold closes. Plastic shoots in. Plastic cools. Mold opens. Parts drop. Mold closes again. Multiply by the hour, by the shift, by the month. That's the business.

Inside the barrel: pellets drop from the hopper, the screw spins, heat bands melt the plastic as the screw pushes it toward the nozzle.

The cycle, step by step

Feed

Pellets fall from the hopper into the barrel. The screw spins and pulls them down into its threads.

Melt

Heat bands on the barrel (300–500°F) melt the pellets as they travel down. By the time the screw has a full shot ready, the plastic is liquid.

Shoot

Mold closes. Screw punches forward. Molten plastic blasts through the nozzle, down the sprue, through the runners, into every cavity.

Cool

Water circulates through channels in the steel. The plastic freezes in the shape of the cavity. Takes most of the cycle time.

Eject

Mold opens. Ejector pins slam forward. Parts drop into a chute or a box (sometimes a water bath). Screw starts turning for the next shot. Repeat.

"By the time it's filled, it's ready to be shot into the mold. It happens really fast because those heating bands are, I think, 300 degrees on the low end and 500 on the high end." — Gary, on how the melt keeps pace with the shot

05

Materials

Not all plastic is plastic.

Every plastic has a personality. Some are cheap and forgiving. Some are expensive and act like metal. Knowing which one to reach for — and which one not to let anywhere near which other one — is most of the job.

Polystyrene (PS) Easy

The cheap one. Low melt temp. Molds like a dream. Recognizable by the way it goes slightly white when you bend it, and the very specific smell.

Think fountain-drink lid caps, disposable stuff, things that don't need to be strong. Affordable, fast to run, very forgiving.

Melt tempLow (~300°F)
CostCheapest tier
StrengthLow · brittle
Use whenDisposable / low-stress parts

Polypropylene (PP) Easy

A bit softer and more flexible than polystyrene. Low melt temp, easy to work with. Good general-purpose plastic.

Still a cost-optimized material. You're not going to build precision mechanical parts out of PP — but for caps, closures, simple housings, it's a workhorse.

Melt tempLow (~350°F)
CostCheap
StrengthLow · flexible
Use whenCaps, housings, flexible parts

Acetal / Celcon Engineered

This is the one we've been running forever. Engineered resin. High heat. Precision. You can replace metal parts with it.

Want a part with a living hinge? A snap fit? A bearing surface? Acetal. The fumes while it's molding are nasty — don't breathe them. More expensive, much higher temperature, but the part does real mechanical work.

Melt tempHigh (400–450°F)
Cost$$$
StrengthHigh · bendable without breaking
Use whenPrecision, hinges, mechanical load

Nylon Engineered · Fussy

Strong, tough, great in parts that see wear. But nylon is a diva about moisture.

Summer: humid. Nylon absorbs water from the air. Moist plastic + heat = steam bubbles in your shot. You need a dryer before molding.

Winter: dry. Parts come out brittle because they have no moisture. After molding, you bag them with a splash of water so they rehydrate before shipping.

Melt tempHigh (450–500°F)
Cost$$$
StrengthVery high · tough
Watch out forMoisture — pre-dry in summer
The Cardinal Rule

Never. Mix. Plastics.

Switching materials on a machine? Clean the grinder spotless. Blow out every hopper. One pellet of polyethylene in a nylon run is a failed lot. One pellet of anything in anything else is a returned order.

06

Runners & Regrind

Nothing goes to waste — carefully.

Every shot makes your parts and a tree of leftover plastic called the runner. Same stuff that connects pieces in a model-airplane kit. The ladies on the line break the parts off the runner and sort them into boxes. If the mold has a tunnel gate, the runner is already separated and it's much less painful.

The runners don't get thrown away. They go to the grinder — big spinning blades in a box — and get chewed up into regrind. The grind drops into a barrel and gets blended back into fresh pellets for the next run.

Regrind means you pay for plastic once but mold it twice. Just be careful: every grinder switchover requires a blowgun and paranoid cleaning. One stray pellet contaminates the next batch.

07

Quoting & Money

Machine time. Cavities. Cycle.

A quote is a prediction. You're promising a price per thousand parts, months from now, with certain assumptions about how fast the machine runs and how many cavities drop parts every shot. Get the math right and there's profit. Get it wrong and you run the job all month for free.

The quote formula

Per-thousand pricing
( machine_time_per_hour
  ÷ ( 3600 ÷ cycle_seconds × cavities ) )
+ material_cost_per_part
+ separation_labor
+ mold_amortization
× 1000
= price per thousand parts
Blanket cost for running the machine: power, labor, floor space, maintenance, depreciation. Different rate for a 28-ton vs. a 40-ton. Different for high-temp materials that wear the screw faster.
Parts per hour = (3600 / cycle in seconds) × cavities. 20-second cycle × 8 cavities = 1,440 parts/hour. That's the throughput number. If one cavity goes down, you're at 1,260 — a silent 12% hit to margin.
Pellet cost × shot weight, amortized across cavities. Regrind lowers this — but only up to ~20% before you're risking part quality. Engineered resins (acetal, nylon) multiply this line 3–5×.
If the mold has a good tunnel gate, the runner self-separates and labor is almost zero. If not, the ladies on the line are breaking parts all day with taped fingers — factor it into the quote.
If Security buys the mold for $15,000 and the customer commits to 100,000 parts, we can add a couple bucks per thousand until the mold is paid off in ~6 months. Even better: Security owns the mold and sells to 3–5 customers. That's a money-printer.

08

Mold Makers

The hardest relationship in the business.

A mold maker turns a blueprint into a hunk of steel that makes parts for years. The best ones think like watchmakers. One of ours actually fixed watches on the weekends. But the relationship? Brutal. Every time.

"If you can work with mold makers, you are a god. They take so much work to work with — to get parts on time, to develop, to deliver molds that are good. I can't emphasize how hard it is." — Gary, not sugar-coating it

The four hard truths

8 weeks. 12 weeks. Three months if the shop is slammed. Customers want it yesterday. The mold maker wants the schedule they already have. You live in between.
The mold arrives. You set it up and shoot the first plastic. Maybe parts stick on the A-side. Maybe pins push through the cavity. Maybe everything works beautifully (rare, suspicious). More often: mold goes back for rework. Another week lost.
You want the machines running paid jobs. So squeezing in a new-mold sample means stopping a running job, changing over, testing, then changing back. Every interruption costs money and sanity.
It's a shrinking craft. The best ones have a waiting list. The cheap ones deliver molds that don't work. Every dollar saved at quote time can come back 10× in sampling rounds.

Gary's long-game wish

Build molds in-house.

Every generation has talked about doing mold-making inside the shop. It's never happened. If you ever figure out how, you'd control your biggest bottleneck and biggest cost line at the same time. Worth thinking about.

Customers & Quality

"We'll take care of it."

Security Molding's parts don't get used — they get mated with something else. A gear goes into a shutter. A cap goes onto an aluminum tube. A fitting snaps onto a window frame. If our part is wrong, the whole assembly fails.

Which means when something goes wrong downstream, everyone's first move is to blame the plastics guy. The aluminum guy's parts cost 5× more to return — so they push hard. The customer's supplier wants a quick answer. You're in the middle, proving your parts were right.

The defense is Total Quality Management — TQM — plus receipts. Dimensional checks on every shift. Documented. Consistent. You don't just know your parts were good — you can show it.

"The standard answer to any problem somebody was having was, 'we'll take care of it.' There's a lot to be said for that." — Gary · on grandpa's operating system

The communication ladder

The more often you check in, the fewer surprises. The fewer surprises, the less you lose to scrap, emergencies, and lost accounts.

Weekly check-in

A 5-min call or email. "How's the stock level? Any upcoming spikes?"

Design review

Before they commit to a drawing, offer a tweak that saves mold cost. Customers love this.

Quality doc

Every lot ships with a simple QC sheet. Receipts.

Something wrong?

Same day. "We'll take care of it" — then actually take care of it.

After action

Follow up after a fix. Ask what you could've done sooner. Write it down.

10

Inventory & JIT

How much to make. When to make it.

Carry too much inventory — it sits on the floor, ties up cash, gets obsolete when the customer changes specs. Carry too little — customer calls for 20,000 parts tomorrow and you're breaking the machine down from another job to squeeze them in. The balance is an art called just-in-time.

Too much

The warehouse pile-up

  • Cash tied up in raw material + labor
  • Floor space eaten by boxes of finished parts
  • Risk: customer changes spec, entire lot is scrap
  • Risk: customer switches supplier, inventory is sunk cost
Too little

The emergency cycle

  • Customer calls — "need 10k by Thursday"
  • Stop a running paid job, change over, eat the overtime
  • Change back, eat the overtime again
  • Repeat every 6 weeks — the whole shop feels it

Gary's hunch

This is a place where AI can actually help.

Usage patterns by customer. Seasonal spikes. Order cadence. Modern software (or a sharp spreadsheet) can forecast demand much better than "we take this much every year." You're walking into a shop that has decades of order history sitting in filing cabinets. That's training data.

Future & Marketing

Tell the story. Run the experiment.

Two things Security Molding has historically been bad at, and both are wide-open opportunities for Julian to own.

Opportunity 01

Run a lab.

Every company Gary respects — Apple, Google — has a lab where engineers run experiments that might fail. Security's never had one. It wouldn't take much: a small budget, a couple of hours a week, and a willingness to try stuff that probably won't work.

Starting list:

  • 3D printing for mold samples (used to be $100k, now it's $2–5k)
  • New engineered resins (there are dozens of 2026-era materials we've never tried)
  • Vacuum-load systems, drying upgrades, automation retrofits
  • AI-driven QC inspection — a camera over the chute
Opportunity 02

Market consistently.

Security doesn't really market. Which means when a slow month shows up, there's no pipeline. Meanwhile: every week of running jobs is visually interesting. Machines. Molds. Little parts falling in a box. Stories.

Starting list:

  • Capture 30 seconds of video every week. Anything.
  • One customer story per month — with permission
  • A simple newsletter to existing accounts. Just "here's what we're up to."
  • Bi-weekly check-in with every top-10 account. Prevents 90% of surprises.
"The more I market, the more business I get. It's just the bottom line." — Gary, admitting the lesson
